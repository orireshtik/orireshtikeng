IP Protection & Digital Assets

Supreme Court victory for performers

Impact: A precedent-setting Supreme Court victory restored statutory compensation for modern digital copying and created multimillion-ILS upside for performers.

Reclaiming rights: the blank media levy dispute

Israel’s private-copying compensation framework had remained anchored in 1996-era analog technologies. Modern digital devices such as smartphones, flash drives and converter boxes were excluded, leaving performers uncompensated while the fund remained frozen at 1.5M ILS.

In 2021, the government attempted to replace the statutory compensation mechanism with discretionary “support tests,” effectively downgrading performers’ legal rights into state support. Eshkolot refused to accept that framework.

The Supreme Court action (Oct 2021)

Filed a decisive petition to the Supreme Court, led by senior legal counsel, demanding cancellation of regulatory workarounds such as support tests, restoration of the statutory compensation mechanism, retroactive payments and creation of a professional committee to update the levy for the digital era.

Government concession and court-backed settlement

Inclusion of modern digital devices

The result was a dramatic reversal: the state backed down and agreed to interpret the law broadly, bringing modern digital recording devices into the compensation framework.

Financial Impact

This legal achievement created a durable economic anchor. Performers now rely on a statutory mechanism expected to generate millions of ILS in legitimate compensation while reducing exposure to arbitrary government decisions.

Current Status

The inter-ministerial committee is now active. We are accompanying its work alongside senior economists and legal experts to ensure the levy reflects current content-consumption patterns and technological reality.

Promoting public, governmental and economic recognition of the contribution of the self-employed to the Israeli economy.

Lahav - the chamber of self-employed organizations in Israel, was established in 1983 to represent, promote and act in the interests of the self-employed sector and small and medium-sized businesses.

Lahav works to protect the rights of the self-employed and small and medium-sized businesses in the economic field, to correct past discriminations, to prevent new distortions and to promote equality in legislation.

Lahav is the representative organization of the self-employed and the small and medium-sized businesses, which make up about 98% of the businesses in Israel and are a major engine of growth in the economy. The sector has over 530,000 self-employed earners and entrepreneurs and employs another 700,000 employees.

The organization counts 71 independent organizations and professional organizations, including about 250,000 members, and works with the state authorities to create an economic environment that supports growth, prosperity and fairness for the self-employed and small and medium-sized businesses.
